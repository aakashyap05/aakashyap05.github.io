What This Lab Demonstrates

This lab sequence shows my ability to work through the full experimental engineering process: instrument a physical system, collect data, reduce measurements into engineering quantities, quantify uncertainty, interpret failure behavior, and communicate conclusions in a structured technical report.

The value of this work was not just applying Mechanics of Materials equations. Each report required engineering judgment: deciding which assumptions were valid, understanding how measurement noise affected the result, comparing independent methods, and explaining what the data could actually support.

Engineering Mindset

Real hardware is never perfect. These labs trained me to think through noise, tolerances, calibration, setup limitations, boundary conditions, and failure modes before trusting a result.

Case Studies

Lab Reports

Lab 1 DAQ / Strain Gauges

Uncertainty Analysis of Strain Gauge Measurements Using Digital Data Acquisition

Estimated the outside radius of a stool footrest using strain-based voltage measurements and compared the result against a direct geometric method. This report focused on measurement resolution, Wheatstone bridge behavior, signal amplification, DAQ window selection, and uncertainty propagation.

Engineering challenge Resolve small strain signals from noisy voltage measurements.
Takeaway Signal conditioning and DAQ setup can control whether a measurement is useful.

Skills: LabVIEW, DAQ, strain gauges, Wheatstone bridge circuits, RSS uncertainty

Lab 2 Beam Bending / Calibration

Strain-Instrumented Cantilever Beam Scale

Built and evaluated a strain-instrumented cantilever beam scale for estimating bottle weight and average gulp size. The report compared a calibration-based voltage-to-weight method against an analytical beam-bending model and used uncertainty analysis to decide which method was more reliable.

Engineering challenge Use measured strain response to estimate applied load.
Takeaway Calibration can outperform a theoretical model when real setup effects dominate.

Skills: beam bending, calibration, strain gauges, Monte Carlo uncertainty, LabVIEW

Lab 3 Material Characterization

Failure Testing of Material Specimens

Used displacement-controlled tensile and compressive testing to generate stress-strain behavior for an unknown metal, carbon fiber, nylon, and plaster of Paris. The report extracted material properties, compared brittle and ductile behavior, evaluated specific strength and stiffness, and identified the unknown metal as most consistent with 7075-T6 aluminum.

Engineering challenge Turn load-displacement data into defensible material behavior.
Takeaway Failure behavior, density, strength, and uncertainty all matter in material ID.

Skills: Instron testing, stress-strain analysis, material identification, Ramberg-Osgood modeling

Lab 4 Adhesive Joint Failure

Failure Analysis of Adhesives

Evaluated shear strength of four adhesives in 6061-T6 aluminum double-lap joints using measured failure load and post-failure adhesive area. The report used ImageJ area measurements, failure-mode observations, load-path assumptions, and uncertainty comparison to evaluate adhesive performance.

Engineering challenge Relate failure load and fractured bond area to adhesive shear strength.
Takeaway Joint performance depends on adhesive selection, surface prep, geometry, and failure mode.

Skills: adhesive joints, ImageJ, shear stress, failure analysis, uncertainty comparison

Final Project Pressure Vessels / Independent Method Comparison

Internal Pressure of a Carbonated Beverage Can

Estimated the internal pressure of an unopened carbonated beverage can using two independent methods: strain gauges aligned with hoop and longitudinal directions, and a diameter-change method after opening. The work applied thin-walled pressure-vessel theory, plane-stress relationships, DAQ measurement, and uncertainty propagation to determine whether the two methods supported a consistent pressure estimate.

Engineering challenge Use measured deformation to estimate pressure inside a thin-walled vessel.
Takeaway Independent methods can support the same conclusion while exposing different error sources.

Skills: strain gauges, DAQ, pressure-vessel theory, plane stress, uncertainty propagation, technical writing

Engineer’s Takeaway

Testing is where assumptions meet hardware.

The biggest takeaway from this lab sequence was learning how much engineering judgment sits between raw data and a final conclusion. The calculations matter, but so do sensor placement, calibration quality, noise, uncertainty, boundary conditions, failure observations, and whether the model assumptions are actually reasonable for the test.

This is the type of engineering work I want to keep building on: testing hardware, understanding how it behaves, quantifying confidence in the result, and communicating the conclusion clearly enough that another engineer can trust it.
