Project Overview

Using CNC manufacturing to turn design intent into real parts.

I completed the America’s Cutting Edge CNC Machining Training Program at the University of Florida, where I built on my previous manual machining experience and continued developing my manufacturing skill set through a hands-on air engine project.

Through the course, I used Autodesk Fusion 360 for CAM and worked with a HAAS Mini Mill to learn more about toolpath planning, workholding, probing, tool offsets, feeds and speeds, and machining different materials including 6061 aluminum, mild steel, and PLA.

One of my biggest takeaways was how much engineering judgment goes into choosing the right manufacturing process. Manual machining builds a strong foundation for setup, material behavior, and cutting conditions, while CNC machining adds precision, repeatability, and scalability.

Project Objective

The objective of the ACE air engine project was to connect design, CAM, machining, inspection, and assembly into one complete manufacturing workflow. Rather than only learning isolated CNC commands, the course showed how a design becomes a manufactured part through decisions about toolpaths, workholding, material selection, machine setup, and inspection.

This experience helped me better understand design for manufacturing. A part may look simple in CAD, but it still has to be fixtured, reached by a tool, machined in a logical order, checked against its requirements, and assembled with other components.

Manufacturing Mindset

Good engineering does not stop at the CAD model. The design has to survive the realities of tooling, setup, tolerances, material behavior, machine limitations, and assembly.

What This Project Demonstrated

CAM Toolpath Planning

Using Fusion 360 to plan how parts get made

I used Autodesk Fusion 360 to understand how CAM toolpaths translate design geometry into machine motion. This included thinking through tool selection, cutting order, material removal, and how the CNC would actually approach the part.

Design for Manufacturing

Choosing a process that fits the part

The course helped me understand that manufacturing is not just about making a part possible; it is about choosing a process that makes the part practical, repeatable, accurate, and safe to produce.

CNC Setup

Workholding, probing, and tool offsets

I learned how setup decisions affect machining success, including workholding strategy, probing, tool offsets, stock positioning, and the importance of controlling variables before pressing cycle start.

Materials and Cutting

Machining different material behaviors

The training exposed me to machining different materials including 6061 aluminum, mild steel, and PLA. This showed how material behavior affects cutting strategy, feeds and speeds, surface finish, and machining decisions.

Engineer’s Takeaway

Manufacturing experience changes how you design.

This project made manufacturing feel less abstract. It showed that engineering decisions become real when material, tools, setup, tolerances, safety, and assembly all have to work together.

Seeing how manual machining and CNC machining complement each other gave me a better appreciation for how engineering designs become real parts. Manual machining builds the hands-on foundation for setup and material behavior, while CNC machining adds precision, repeatability, and scalability.
